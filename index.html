How does it affect human health?

Cardiovascular Health

Diets high in marine-derived omega-3 fats improve heart health.

Studies show that people with the highest fish intake have ~15% lower risk of heart failure than those with the lowest intake.



Djoussé, L., Akinkuolie, A. O., Wu, J. H., Ding, E. L., & Gaziano, J. M. (2012). Fish consumption, omega-3 fatty acids and risk of heart failure: a meta-analysis. Clinical Nutrition, 31(6), 846-853.

Nutrition

Fish and seafood are dietary cornerstones for Filipinos. The Philippines consumes ~40 kg of fish per person annually, making it the top source of animal protein.

In the Philippines, seafood is a cheap and accessible source of protein, rich in micronutrients, such as iron, zinc, vitamin A, and omega 3 fatty acids. As such, seafood has significant potential to contribute towards alleviating food insecurity, malnutrition, stunting, and cardiovascular disease, while strengthening the immune system and improving maternal and childhood health outcomes.


MRAG Asia Pacific (2020). The State of Fish in Nutrition Systems in the Philippines. 87p.

Brain Development and Mental Health

Fish consumption provides docosahexaenoic acid (DHA), an omega-3 fatty acid essential for brain development in infants and cognitive function in adults. Adequate intake during pregnancy and early childhood is linked to improved cognitive outcomes.

Also, regular fish consumption (≥2 servings/week) is linked to 20% lower depression risk and improved cognitive function. Omega-3 fatty acids (DHA/EPA) reduce neuroinflammation and support neurotransmitter activity.


Mischoulon, D., MD PhD. (2020, October 27). Omega-3 fatty acids for mood disorders. Harvard Health. https://www.health.harvard.edu/blog/omega-3-fatty-acids-for-mood-disorders-2018080314414

Oxygen Production

Algae (including phytoplankton) generate 50-80% of Earth's oxygen through photosynthesis—more than all rainforests combined.

This oxygen supports life both in oceans and on land, contributing significantly to air quality and respiratory health. Their oxygen production also drives the global carbon cycle, which indirectly moderates climate and associated respiratory stressors


How much oxygen comes from the ocean? (n.d.). https://oceanservice.noaa.gov/facts/ocean-oxygen.html

Impact Details

Ocean Acidification occurs when seawater absorbs carbon dioxide from the atmosphere, making it more acidic.

• pH levels have dropped by 0.1 units since pre-industrial times

• Shell-forming organisms like mollusks and corals struggle to build protective structures

• Food chain disruption affects fish populations and marine ecosystems

• Economic impact: $1 trillion loss to global fisheries by 2100


Source: NOAA Ocean Acidification Program (2023)

Coral Bleaching happens when corals expel their symbiotic algae due to stress, turning white.

• Temperature rise of just 1-2°C triggers massive bleaching events

• 50% of shallow-water corals have been lost in the past 30 years

• Recovery time can take 10-15 years under optimal conditions

• Biodiversity loss: 25% of marine species depend on coral reefs


Source: Australian Institute of Marine Science (2023)

Overfishing depletes fish stocks faster than they can naturally replenish.

• 90% of large predatory fish have been eliminated since 1950

• Fishing quotas exceeded by 3x in many commercial areas

• Ecosystem collapse disrupts entire marine food webs

• Economic dependency: 820 million people rely on fisheries for livelihood


Source: FAO State of World Fisheries Report (2022)

Plastic Pollution introduces synthetic materials that persist in marine environments for centuries.

• 8 million tons of plastic enter oceans annually

• Microplastics found in 83% of marine species studied

• Chemical leaching disrupts hormone systems in marine life

• Great Pacific Garbage Patch now twice the size of Texas


Source: Ocean Conservancy Marine Debris Report (2023)
